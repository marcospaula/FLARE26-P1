\title{Knowing When the Answer Isn't There:\\
Ontology-Gated Extraction for False-Positive-Free Multi-Document Audit}
\author{Marcos de Paula \\ FLARE26 Project}
\date{Working draft --- applied / systems paper}

\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems applied to document comparison tend
to \emph{hallucinate} an answer when the requested information is simply absent,
producing \textbf{false-positive divergences} that erode trust in audit
settings---where the costliest error is not getting a value wrong, but inventing
a disagreement that does not exist. We present FLARE26, a neuro-symbolic
``glass-box'' auditor whose extraction step is \textbf{ontologically gated}: the
model must explicitly declare the \emph{type} and the \emph{scope} of both the
question and the retrieved evidence, and abstains (returns an explicit
\emph{evidence gap}) whenever they do not match. A downstream
\textbf{deterministic} judge then decides consensus/divergence across $N$
documents over the typed, abstained outputs. On a 30-pair benchmark of Brazilian
contracts and tender notices, ontological gating \textbf{cuts the false-positive
divergence rate from $38\%$ to the low single digits} ($\sim$1--2\% per
extraction; a $\sim$20--30$\times$ reduction), and a \textbf{self-consistency}
vote exposes an explicit, tunable precision/recall operating curve: increasing
the number of samples $k$ raises answer recall ($81\% \to 93\%$) at the price of
a small rise in false positives ($\sim$1\% $\to$ $\sim$5\%). The gate's advantage
over the baseline is large and robust; the residual false positives are rare
leaks rather than zero, which we characterize honestly rather than claim away.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Organizations increasingly compare large numbers of structured-but-unstructured
documents---contracts, tender notices (\emph{editais}), financial
statements---to detect discrepancies. A natural approach is RAG: retrieve
relevant passages and ask an LLM to extract the answer per document, then
compare. This breaks down in a specific, costly way: \textbf{standard RAG prefers
answering to abstaining.} When a contract is asked ``what is the penalty for
\emph{total non-performance}?'' but only contains a clause about
\emph{late-payment} penalty, a naive extractor returns the late-payment
value---fabricating an answer to a question the document never addresses. Across
documents, such fabrications manifest as \textbf{false-positive divergences}: a
flagged disagreement that is an artifact of hallucination.

In auditing, the cost structure differs from open-domain QA. A false positive
triggers unnecessary legal review or a wrong decision; it is worse than an honest
``not found, please verify.'' We therefore target the false-positive divergence
rate as the primary metric.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(1) \textbf{Ontologically-gated extraction} with explicit abstention: the
extractor declares the \emph{type} and \emph{scope} of question vs.\ evidence and
abstains on mismatch. (2) A \textbf{deterministic $N$-way judge} over the typed
outputs, auditable and $O(N)$. (3) A \textbf{benchmark and metric} for
ontological abstention on Brazilian legal documents, showing a
\textbf{$\sim$20--30$\times$ reduction in false-positive divergences} and an
explicit, tunable recall\,$\times$\,precision curve via self-consistency.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Retrieval-augmented generation} \cite{lewis2020rag,gao2023ragsurvey}
grounds generation in retrieved passages; our concern is the failure mode where
generation proceeds despite absent in-scope evidence. \textbf{Structured
extraction from LLMs} (function-calling, Instructor \cite{instructor}, Guardrails
\cite{guardrails}, constrained decoding \cite{willard2023guided}) coerces outputs
into typed schemas; we make the schema's primary job an \emph{abstention
pre-condition}. \textbf{Selective prediction / abstention}
\cite{geifman2017selective} trades coverage for reliability; LLMs partially know
what they know \cite{kadavath2022know}; the reading-comprehension analogue is
answerability \cite{rajpurkar2018squad2}, closest in spirit to our gate. We frame
abstention as a \emph{pre-condition for cross-document comparison} rather than a
post-hoc QA filter. \textbf{Faithfulness / hallucination}
\cite{ji2023survey,maynez2020faithfulness} and RAG evaluation \cite{es2024ragas}
ask whether an answer is supported; we target the dual failure of answering when
no in-scope evidence exists. \textbf{Legal NLP}
\cite{hendrycks2021cuad,chalkidis2020legalbert} provides contract-review datasets
and models; we contribute an abstention-centered evaluation in Brazilian
Portuguese public-procurement text.

\section{Method}
FLARE26 is a pipeline: hybrid retrieval (M1.5) $\to$ ontologically-gated
extraction (M2) $\to$ deterministic $N$-way judge (M4) $\to$ executive synthesis
(M5). Every verdict carries provenance to the source span (``glass box'').

\subsection{Ontologically-gated extraction (M2)}
The extractor emits a typed record separating \textbf{two independent checks}:
\textbf{Type/instrument} (the named instrument the question asks about, e.g.\ a
\emph{penalty} vs.\ \emph{interest}---distinct instruments even when both are
percentages; must match) and \textbf{Scope/condition} (the specific
condition/event the value applies to; mismatched scopes $\Rightarrow$ abstain).
If either check fails, or a negative user restriction is violated, the record
collapses to an explicit evidence gap with confidence $0$. The compatibility rule
is \textbf{domain-agnostic}: stated over abstract instruments $P/Q$ and
conditions $A/B$, with no hard-coded legal terms.

\subsection{Deterministic $N$-way judge (M4)}
Given the typed answer per document, the judge groups documents by
\textbf{equivalent answer} (Brazilian number normalization: \texttt{R\$
1.200.000,50} $\to$ \texttt{1200000.5}; \texttt{12,5\%} $\equiv$ \texttt{12.5}).
The verdict is \emph{consensus} (one group), \emph{divergence} (multiple
groups), or \emph{evidence gap}, in $O(N)$ with no LLM call. Since the judge sees
only abstained/typed outputs, an upstream hallucination is the only way to create
a false-positive divergence---exactly what the gate suppresses.

\subsection{Self-consistency (optional)}
The extractor is run $k$ times; the system answers if \textbf{any} run answers,
abstaining only if all $k$ abstain, returning the most frequent value.
Section~\ref{sec:sc} explains why this asymmetric policy is safe.

\section{Evaluation}
\paragraph{Corpus \& gold.} 30 (question, document) pairs over 16 documents and 7
questions, from Brazilian service contracts and public tender notices. Each pair
is labeled with the correct answer \textbf{or} \texttt{ABSTAIN} when the document
lacks the requested datum (13 ABSTAIN, 17 answerable); labels verified against
source text. Discriminating cases are included by design: \emph{interest $\neq$
penalty}, \emph{warranty term $\neq$ payment term}, \emph{total non-performance
$\neq$ late payment}.

\paragraph{Systems.} (i) BASELINE: free extraction, no gate. (ii) FLARE26: gated
extraction (strict scope). (iii) FLARE26 + self-consistency ($k=5$). Extractor:
gpt-4o-mini, temperature $0$, fixed seed.

\paragraph{Metrics.} (a) \textbf{False-positive rate}: fraction of ABSTAIN items
the system answers (headline). (b) \textbf{Abstention recall}: fraction of
ABSTAIN items correctly abstained. (c) \textbf{Answer recall}: fraction of
answerable items answered. Numbers are mean $\pm$ std (BASELINE: 3 runs;
FLARE26-strict: 8 runs; self-consistency: bootstrap $k=5$, 300 resamples).

\section{Results}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Main result (30 pairs; 13 ABSTAIN, 17 answerable). Mean $\pm$ std.}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
System & False-positive $\downarrow$ & Abst.\ recall $\uparrow$ & Answer recall $\uparrow$ \\
\midrule
BASELINE (free extraction)        & $38\% \pm 0\%$ & $62\% \pm 0\%$ & $100\% \pm 0\%$ \\
FLARE26 (single-call gating)      & $\sim$1--2\% & $\sim$98--100\% & $81\% \pm 6\%$ \\
FLARE26 + self-consistency ($k{=}5$) & $\sim$3\% & $\sim$97\% & $91\% \pm 3\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Ontological gating cuts the false-positive divergence rate from $38\%$ to the low
single digits ($\sim$1--2\% per extraction; an 8-run sample measured $0\%$, but a
larger-pool bootstrap (\S\ref{sec:sc}) places the true rate at $\sim$1--2\%---the
$0\%$ was within sampling noise of a rare event), correctly handling the
\emph{interest$\neq$penalty}, \emph{warranty$\neq$payment} and
\emph{non-performance$\neq$late-payment} distinctions. The residual false
positives are \textbf{rare leaks, not zero}; we report the full precision/recall
operating curve in \S\ref{sec:sc}.

\subsection{Ablation: precision/recall frontier of the scope rule}
\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Scope rule & False-positive & Answer recall \\
\midrule
Strict (condition equality) & $\mathbf{0\%}$ & $71$--$82\%$ \\
Intersection (subset/superset) & $38\%$ & $88\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
Loosening scope to accept sub/superset conditions recovers recall but
\textbf{reopens false positives} (a ``late-payment penalty'' is returned for a
``non-performance penalty'' question). The strict rule is the correct operating
point: never asserting a false divergence outweighs recall.

\subsection{Error analysis}
Residual abstentions on answerable items are either (i) a \emph{legitimate
frontier}---evidence strictly more specific (\texttt{delay $>$ 10 days} for
``delay'') or more general (\texttt{partial or total default} for ``total'')
than the question---or (ii) \emph{non-determinism} (e.g.\ ``retention 5\%/10\%''
oscillates), addressed next.

\subsection{The recall $\times$ cost operating curve of self-consistency}
\label{sec:sc}
We run the extractor up to 10 times per item, collect the answer pool, and
bootstrap the ``answer if $\geq 1$ of $k$'' decision (400 resamples), yielding an
explicit precision/recall operating curve in $k$:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
$k$ (LLM calls/doc) & False-positive & Answer recall \\
\midrule
1  & $1\% \pm 2\%$ & $81\% \pm 6\%$ \\
2  & $1\% \pm 3\%$ & $87\% \pm 5\%$ \\
3  & $2\% \pm 3\%$ & $89\% \pm 5\%$ \\
5  & $3\% \pm 4\%$ & $91\% \pm 3\%$ \\
8  & $5\% \pm 4\%$ & $93\% \pm 2\%$ \\
10 & $5\% \pm 4\%$ & $93\% \pm 2\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
Two honest observations. First, \textbf{ABSTAIN leakage is rare but nonzero}
($\sim$1--2\% per sample). Second, the permissive ``$\geq 1$ of $k$'' vote
\emph{amplifies} it as $k$ grows, so recall ($81\% \to 93\%$) and false positives
($1\% \to 5\%$) rise together---a genuine trade-off, \textbf{not a free lunch}
(an earlier, smaller experiment that observed $0$ leakage was within the sampling
noise of this low rate). Recall saturates near $93\%$ ($k \geq 8$); the remaining
gap is the legitimate scope frontier. A stricter threshold ($\geq t$ of $k$) moves
along the curve toward fewer false positives; the full $(k,t)$ sweep is future
work. Throughout, the gate stays far below the $38\%$ baseline---the paper's
central, robust claim.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\textbf{Trade-off, not a guarantee:} the gate does not reach exactly $0\%$ false
positives; it leaks rarely ($\sim$1--2\%) and self-consistency trades a small
false-positive rise for recall---the defensible claim is the $\sim$20--30$\times$
reduction vs.\ baseline. \textbf{Small benchmark:} with only 13 ABSTAIN items a
$\sim$1--2\% rate cannot be estimated tightly; a larger benchmark is needed and
\emph{strengthens} the evaluation. \textbf{External validity:} the corpus is
partly synthetic and clean; on messy real tender notices (present but not yet
labeled) the rate may rise, though likely still far below $38\%$---testing this is
the priority. LLM non-determinism is mitigated by seed $+$ self-consistency
(mean $\pm$ std with bootstrap). The extractor depends on a proprietary LLM;
open-model replication is future work. This is an \textbf{architectural pattern},
not a new ML method.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
In multi-document audit, the dangerous error is the fabricated disagreement.
Gating extraction on explicit type$+$scope compatibility, with deterministic
downstream comparison, \textbf{cuts the false-positive divergence rate by
$\sim$20--30$\times$} ($38\%$ to low single digits); self-consistency then exposes
a \textbf{tunable recall\,$\times$\,precision operating curve} ($k$ from 1 to 10:
recall $81\% \to 93\%$, false positives $\sim$1\% $\to$ $\sim$5\%). The remaining
false positives are rare leaks we quantify rather than claim away. Future work: a
larger, double-annotated benchmark (including real tender notices) to tighten the
estimate, the full $(k,t)$ vote sweep, and open-model replication.
